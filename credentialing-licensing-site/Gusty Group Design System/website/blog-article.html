Credentialing

The real cost of a credentialing delay — and how to model it.

Every week a provider sits un-credentialed is revenue you never bill back. Here's how to put a number on it, prioritize your roster by revenue-at-risk, and shorten the path to a clean file.

Dana Ellison · Head of Credentialing
Jul 14, 2026 · 8 min read

When a practice hires a new clinician, the celebration is real — and the clock is already running. From the day that offer is signed to the day the provider can bill an in-network visit, there is a window where the provider is on payroll but not producing revenue. Most practices underestimate how wide that window is, and almost none put a dollar figure on it.

That figure matters. Industry estimates put the revenue at risk from credentialing and enrollment delays at up to $100,000 per provider. The good news: once you can model the cost, you can manage it — and prioritize the work that recovers the most revenue first.

Where the money actually leaks

Credentialing delays rarely come from a single failure. They accumulate across small, avoidable gaps: an incomplete CAQH profile, a license that lapsed mid-application, a payer packet returned for a missing signature. Each restarts a queue that can run 90 to 180 days.

  • Idle salary. The provider is paid while unable to bill in-network.
  • Lost visit volume. Every slot that could have been billed is gone, not deferred.
  • Out-of-network write-offs. Visits seen before enrollment are often uncollectible.
  • Re-work. A rejected application means starting the payer's clock over.
The cost of a delay isn't the paperwork — it's the visits that never got billed while the paperwork sat in a queue.

A simple model you can run today

You don't need a data team to estimate revenue-at-risk. Start with three inputs per provider: expected weekly billable visits, average reimbursement per visit, and estimated weeks to full enrollment. Multiply them, and you have the exposure for that provider.

Rank the roster, don't work it alphabetically

The instinct is to process providers in the order they were hired. The better move is to rank by revenue-at-risk and work the highest-exposure files first — a high-volume specialist waiting on one commercial payer usually outranks three part-time providers combined.

Key takeaway

Model exposure per provider, sort the roster by dollars-at-risk, and attack the top of the list first. Prioritization alone often recovers weeks of billable time with no extra headcount.

Shortening the window

Once you know where the exposure sits, most of the shortening comes from discipline: build CAQH cleanly the first time, verify credentials at the source before payers ask, and keep a follow-up cadence on every pending application instead of waiting for the payer to respond.

  1. Build a provider-by-payer matrix and prioritize by revenue-at-risk.
  2. Complete CAQH and primary-source verification up front, not reactively.
  3. Submit clean packets and follow up on a fixed schedule.
  4. Monitor continuously so nothing lapses mid-cycle.

The bottom line

Credentialing delays feel like an administrative nuisance, but they are a revenue problem wearing an operations costume. Put a number on the exposure, work the roster by dollars, and treat the enrollment timeline as something you forecast — not something that happens to you.
