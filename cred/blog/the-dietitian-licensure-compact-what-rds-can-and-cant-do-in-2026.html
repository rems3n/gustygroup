The Dietitian Licensure Compact: What RDs Can (and Can't) Do in 2026

The Dietitian Licensure Compact is enacted and activated — but not issuing privileges yet. Here's the real timeline and what it won't fix. keywords: dietitian licensure compact, RD multistate license, dietitian compact states, telehealth dietitian across state lines

GG
Gusty Group ·
Jul 21, 2026 · 5 min read

Quick answer: Yes, the Dietitian Licensure Compact exists. It’s been enacted in 17 states and has reached activation status. But compact privileges are not being issued yet, and the first ones are expected around 2027. Until then, if you see clients across state lines, you still need an individual license in every state that licenses dietitians. Planning your 2026 growth around the compact will leave you unable to practice where you promised clients you could.

What the compact will actually confer

The compact follows the same architecture as the nursing, physical therapy, and psychology compacts: a privilege to practice in remote member states, based on a single home-state license.

This distinction matters and is worth being precise about. A compact privilege is not:

  • A second full license. You are not licensed in the remote state; you hold a privilege to practice there.
  • A national license. There is no such thing for dietitians, and the compact does not create one.
  • Automatic. Once live, you will apply for a privilege in each member state where you want to practice, and pay for it.

What you get is one home-state license plus a privilege in each member state; instead of a full license application, fee, and renewal cycle in every state.

It is worth contrasting this with the physician model. The Interstate Medical Licensure Compact does the opposite: it gives physicians an expedited path to multiple full licenses, each renewing separately. The dietitian compact is a privilege model, which is simpler to maintain - one license, one CE requirement, one renewal - but entirely dependent on that home-state license staying active.

Where it stands right now

The compact has cleared its legislative threshold but not its operational one.

  • Enacted in 17 states as of July 2026: Alabama, Arkansas, Iowa, Kansas, Kentucky, Mississippi, Montana, Nebraska, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Tennessee, Utah, Washington, and Wisconsin.
  • Activation status reached, which formed the Dietitian Licensure Compact Commission — the body that writes the rules, builds the application system, and coordinates among member states.
  • Privileges are not being issued. The commission is still in implementation.
  • Implementation typically runs 12 to 24 months from activation for compacts of this type. The compact's own guidance points to the first privileges being issued sometime in 2027.

More states are actively moving. Kentucky and Washington both enacted in 2026, and additional bills are working through other legislatures, including South Carolina. The member list will look meaningfully different by the time privileges go live.

Compact membership as of July 2026. Source: Council of State Governments National Center for Interstate Compacts. Enacted states are not yet issuing privileges.

Because this changes every legislative session, verify current status with the compact commission before relying on it for any practice decision.

Enacted is not the same as issuing

This is the single most consequential distinction, and it is where dietitians planning multi-state growth go wrong.

Enacted means a state legislature passed the compact into law. It is a commitment to participate.

Issuing means the compact's systems are live, the commission is processing applications, and a dietitian can actually receive a privilege.

Right now, no state is issuing dietitian compact privileges — regardless of what any state legislature has passed. A dietitian in a state that enacted the compact in 2024 has exactly the same practical options today as one in a state that has not enacted it at all.

You will see this pattern across every compact. Counselors watched roughly 40 states enact their compact while only a handful could actually issue privileges. Social workers are in the same position now. The legislative map and the operational map are different maps, and only one of them determines what you can do on Monday morning.

Who will be eligible when it goes live

As designed, eligibility will require:

  • The RD/RDN credential in good standing.
  • An active, unencumbered license in your home state — no disciplinary action, no restrictions.
  • A home state that is a compact member. The compact anchors to your home state; if it is not a member, you cannot use the compact regardless of where you want to practice.
  • A criminal background check. This is a compact requirement.

Once live, you will apply through the commission, pay an administrative fee, and satisfy any additional requirement the remote state imposes — potentially including a jurisprudence exam or state fe, following the pattern other compacts use.

The gaps that will still exist after it launches

The compact will not solve the problem for everyone, and some of these limits are structural rather than temporary.

States that do not license dietitians cannot participate. This is the big one. A compact works by mutual recognition of licenses. If a state does not issue dietitian licenses at all, there is nothing to recognize. States that currently do not license or certify dietitians include California, Colorado, Virginia, Michigan, and Arizona. Dietitians based in those states get no benefit from the compact. If you live in California and want compact access, your only route would be obtaining a license in a compact member state and making it your home state; which means meeting that state's residency and licensure requirements.

The background-check requirement is blocking some states. Several states do not require criminal background checks for dietitian licensure and are not planning to add one. Maryland is the frequently cited example: advocates were told the state would not participate because it does not require background checks. Every state facing that requirement has to change its licensure statute to join, which slows adoption.

Continuing education follows your home state. Once privileges are live, you meet CE requirements in your home state only. That is a genuine simplification, but it means your home state's CE rules govern your entire practice.

Your privilege dies with your home license. This is the dependency people forget. If your home-state license lapses, is suspended, or is restricted, every privilege built on it collapses at the same time. Under a multi-license model, one lapse affects one state. Under a privilege model, one lapse affects everything.

What to do between now and 2027

If you are expanding into new states before the compact goes live, you have one path: individual state licensure in each state that licenses dietitians. There is no workaround.

A practical sequence:

  1. Identify the states you actually need - where your clients are, or where a referral source or employer is sending you. Not a wish list.
  2. Check whether each state licenses dietitians at all. Some do not, which changes the analysis entirely.
  3. Pull each state's requirements: application, fees, jurisprudence exam, background check, supervised practice verification.
  4. Start the applications on a realistic timeline. State licensure commonly takes several weeks to several months depending on the board's backlog.
  5. Build the renewal calendar as you go. Each license carries its own expiration and CE cycle. Tracking three or more manually is how lapses happen.
  6. Track compact progress separately - but do not let it delay a license you need this year.

If the compact goes live in 2027 and covers states where you already hold individual licenses, you may be able to consolidate then. That is an optimization to make later, not a reason to wait now.

Licensure is not credentialing

Worth stating plainly, because it produces real revenue gaps: a license lets you legally practice in a state. It does not put you in network with any payer there.

Even after the compact is issuing, you will still need payer enrollment in each state where you intend to bill insurance. Licensure and credentialing are parallel tracks with different timelines, different applications, and different owners. A dietitian who gets a compact privilege in a new state and starts scheduling insured clients will find those claims denied as out-of-network.

FAQ

Which states have joined? The list changes every legislative session. Check dietitianscompact.org for the current map and pending legislation.

Can I use the compact today? No. It is activated but not issuing. First privileges are expected around 2027.

Does it help California, Colorado, Virginia, Michigan, or Arizona dietitians? No. Those states do not license dietitians, so they cannot participate in a compact built on license recognition.

What if my home state is not a member? You cannot use the compact from a non-member home state. The home-state license is the foundation of the privilege.

Will the compact get me on insurance panels? No. Licensure and payer credentialing are entirely separate.

Do I still need CE in every state? Once privileges are live, CE follows your home state only. Until then, each individual license carries its own CE requirement.

What happens if my home-state license lapses? Every compact privilege built on it collapses with it. Monitor the home license as your most critical credential.

Is this the same as the nursing compact? Structurally similar. Both are privilege models, but note that the nurse compact covers RN/LPN scope only and does not extend to NP practice. Each compact has its own scope limits.

Should I wait for the compact before expanding? No. If you need a state in 2026, license there in 2026.

The takeaway: The Dietitian Licensure Compact is coming, not here. Plan multi-state licensure the traditional way through 2026, and treat the compact as a future simplification rather than a current strategy.
