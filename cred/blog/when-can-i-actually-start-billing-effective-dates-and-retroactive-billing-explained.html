"When Can I Actually Start Billing?" Effective Dates and Retroactive Billing, Explained

Being "approved" for a payer panel isn't the same as being able to bill. Your effective date determines when you can actually see insurance patients. Target keywords: insurance credentialing effective date, can I bill retroactively insurance, when can I see insurance patients

GG
Gusty Group ·
Jul 21, 2026 · 5 min read

Quick answer: Your effective date, not your approval date, is when you can actually bill a payer for services. Medicare will generally honor an effective date you request up to 30 days prior to your application receipt date. Most commercial payers do not backdate; the effective date is often tied to contract execution or, for group-linked providers, the date you're added to the group's roster. Medicaid rules vary by state, but many states honor effective dates prior to application date.

Why "approved" doesn't mean "billable"

Providers hear "approved" and assume they're open for business. They're not. Getting in-network is actually three separate milestones, and only the last one lets you send a claim:

  • Credentialing - the payer verifies your license, education, work history, malpractice coverage, and background. This is the primary-source verification phase.
  • Contracting - once you pass credentialing, the payer issues a participation agreement with your fee schedule. You are not in-network until it's countersigned.
  • Effective date and system load - the payer enters you into its claims system with a specific start date. Only services on or after that date pay in-network.

You can clear credentialing and contracting and still sit unbillable for weeks while the effective date is set and your record is loaded. See how credentialing and contracting differ, and general payer timelines, in our post "How Long Does Insurance Credentialing Take".

The effective date is the only one of the three that controls your revenue.

How effective dates work, by payer type

Medicare is the most generous on backdating. It allows retrospective billing for services furnished up to 30 days before your effective date, and up to 90 days in certain declared disaster or public-health-emergency circumstances. The effective date itself is generally the receipt date of the application that was ultimately approved, so a clean CMS-855I filed through your MAC can produce a small billable window that predates your formal approval. This predictability is the exception, not the rule.

Commercial payers (BCBS, Aetna, Cigna, UnitedHealthcare/Optum) are the strictest. Most set your effective date at contract execution (the day the agreement is countersigned and loaded) and will not backdate to your application date or your start date at the practice. A handful will grant a retroactive date case-by-case, but usually only where there's a documented network-adequacy need or an internal payer error, so plan as if they won't. For providers joining an existing group contract, the date is typically when you're linked to the group's roster, not the day you first saw patients.

Medicaid varies the most, because it's run state by state. Many states are genuinely generous, granting retroactive effective dates back to the application date or earlier. The catch is that managed Medicaid (MCO) plans layer their own credentialing and their own effective dates on top of the state enrollment, and those two dates rarely line up. Confirm both the state enrollment date and each MCO's date before you bill. See our full guide to Medicaid credentialing.

Switching states or switching group affiliation?

Being credentialed with a payer once doesn't lock in a favorable effective date forever. The relationship is tied to a specific NPI, Tax ID, and location. Change any of them and you usually start a new clock:

  • New state: a fresh credentialing and contracting relationship, with a fresh, non-retroactive effective date. See multi-state telehealth credentialing.
  • New group, or leaving one: your panel status usually lives on the group's contract and Tax ID, not on you personally. So moving practices means being re-linked or re-credentialed, and the new effective date does not backdate to cover the gap. See what happens to your panels when you leave a group.

Providers routinely assume "I'm already in-network with Aetna" will carry over. The credential itself may speed things up; the effective date almost never travels with you.

What to actually do before scheduling insurance patients

  • Get the effective date in writing. A verbal "you're all set" from a rep is not a date. Ask for it on the executed contract or in a dated email with a reference number.
  • Confirm what kind of date it is. Contract-execution, group-roster-link, or system-load. These can differ by days or weeks, and only the loaded date actually pays.
  • Don't treat approval as billable. Approval clears credentialing; billing needs the effective date and the system load behind it.
  • For group-linked providers, confirm the roster update actually processed. A submitted roster change is not a completed one. Verify the payer shows you active under the group's Tax ID.
  • Track it per payer. Every payer in a single application round can land on a different effective date, so keep a simple per-payer log rather than assuming one date covers all.

What happens if you see patients before your actual effective date

Claims for dates of service before your true effective date are typically denied outright, and the damage doesn't stop at the denial:

  • You generally can't go back and bill those sessions once the date is finally set (Medicare and some Medicaid programs are the occasional exceptions).
  • Under many in-network contracts you also can't balance-bill the patient for the payer's share, so the revenue is simply gone.
  • Building a caseload against a date you don't have yet becomes a stack of unbillable sessions fast; the hidden cost behind what an unpaneled hire actually costs you.

There are two legitimate bridges for the waiting period. Superbills let patients with out-of-network benefits seek their own reimbursement while you wait (see how superbills work). And in some states, expedited (or provisional) credentialing lets an established group bill under its existing contract while your individual application is still pending (see billing under a group contract while you're still being credentialed).

Neither is automatic, but both beat seeing patients against a date that doesn't exist yet.

FAQ

Can I ask a payer to backdate my effective date if I already saw patients?

Sometimes. Medicare is the most flexible; it allows retrospective billing for up to 30 days before your effective date (more in declared emergencies). Some Medicaid programs backdate to the application date. Most commercial payers decline unless there was a documented payer error or a network-adequacy need, so never build a plan around it.

Can I bill retroactively for the credentialing waiting period?

Usually not with commercial payers. Their effective date is the contract-and-load date, and everything before it is treated as out-of-network. Your realistic options for that window are superbills for patients with OON benefits, cash-pay, or (in some states) expedited credentialing under a group contract.

If I'm added to a group contract, is my effective date the same as my start date at the practice?

Not necessarily; and usually not. Your effective date is the day the payer links you to the group's roster and loads you, which commonly lands 30-90 days after your first day at the practice. Confirm the linked date with each payer before scheduling insurance sessions.

Does my effective date differ by payer even within the same application round?

Yes, often by weeks. Each payer runs its own credentialing, contracting, and system loading on its own clock, so one batch of applications can produce a wide spread of effective dates. Track them individually.

When can I actually start seeing insurance patients?

On or after your loaded effective date for that specific payer; not on your approval date, and not on your practice start date. Until that date, a patient carrying that insurance is effectively out-of-network with you.

Who confirms my effective date?

The payer, in writing; regardless of who filed the application or whether you used a credentialing service. Get it on the executed contract or in a dated message with a reference number, and keep it on file.

The takeaway: "Approved" is not the same question as "billable." Always get the actual effective date in writing before scheduling a single insurance-covered session.
