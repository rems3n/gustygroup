How Long Does Insurance Credentialing Actually Take? (And What Speeds It Up)

Insurance credentialing takes 60–120 days for commercial payers, 30-90 for Medicare, and 30–180 for Medicaid. Here's what speeds it up—and what quietly adds months. Jul 21, 2026 · 5 min read

Quick answer: Insurance credentialing typically takes 60–120 days for commercial payers (Aetna, BCBS, Cigna, UnitedHealthcare), 30–90 days for Medicare, and anywhere from 30-180 days for Medicaid depending on your state. Anyone promising "2 weeks" is describing something other than credentialing.

Every credentialing company promises fast. Almost none of them explain what "fast" actually means, because the honest answer is that most of the timeline sits with the payer, not with whoever files your application. What you can control is whether you land at the short end of those ranges or the long end. The difference is often 90 days of revenue.

The real timelines, by payer type

  • Commercial payers (BCBS, Aetna, Cigna, UHC/Optum): 60–120 days from complete application to effective date. Larger payers in saturated markets trend longer.
  • Medicare: 30-90 days once your PECOS application is complete and your MAC picks it up.
  • Medicaid: 30–180 days. State-run programs vary wildly; managed Medicaid plans add a second credentialing layer on top.

These clocks start when the payer has a complete application — not when you hit submit.

Credentialing vs. contracting: the two-phase reality nobody explains

What most people call "credentialing" is actually two sequential processes:

  1. Credentialing - the payer verifies your license, education, work history, malpractice coverage, and background. This is the primary-source verification phase.
  2. Contracting - after approval, the payer issues a participation agreement with your fee schedule. You are not in-network until this is countersigned and loaded, with an effective date.

Providers get burned by celebrating at the end of phase 1. You can be "approved" and still unable to bill for 3–6 more weeks while contracting and system loading happen. Always ask for your effective date in writing before seeing insurance patients.

The 3 things that genuinely compress timelines

  1. A complete document package on day one. Every missing document triggers a "development request" - and your file goes to the back of a queue each time. A complete first submission is the single biggest lever you have.
  2. Clean data matching across NPPES, CAQH, and your application. Your name, address, taxonomy code, and Tax ID must match exactly across every system. One mismatched suite number can add 30 days.
  3. Scheduled follow-up. Applications stall silently. Calling the payer every 2–3 weeks (and documenting the reference number each time) catches stalls before they become months.

The 3 things that quietly add months

  1. A lapsed CAQH attestation. CAQH requires re-attestation roughly every 120 days. If it lapses mid-review, payers pause your file without notifying you.
  2. Taxonomy mismatch. If the taxonomy code on your NPI doesn't match your license type or your application, most payers reject or shelve the file.
  3. Missed development requests. Payers send document requests to whatever email or fax is on file; often with 10–15 day deadlines. Miss one and the application may be administratively closed, restarting the entire clock.

FAQ

Can I see insurance patients while credentialing is pending?

Generally no. Claims for dates of service before your effective date are denied. Some payers allow retroactive effective dates; most commercial payers don't. Don't build a caseload on hope.

Does hiring a credentialing company make it faster?

A credentialing company like Gusty Group prevents the self-inflicted delays: incomplete packets, lapsed attestations, missed follow-ups, etc. These account for most timeline delays.

What's the fastest payer to get credentialed with?

Medicare, ironically, is often the most predictable (30-90 days) because it's one standardized process. Commercial payers vary by region and panel status.
