Quick answer: RBTs are generally not credentialed with payers the way BCBAs are, but that does not mean there are no requirements. RBT time is billable only when the technician holds current certification, works under documented BCBA supervision, and the claim is submitted the way that specific payer requires. Some payers do require RBTs on the roster. "Not credentialed" is not the same as "no rules."

## The short version

Most payers treat the RBT as a rendering provider whose services are billed under the practice and the directing BCBA, rather than as an independently contracted provider. The RBT does not typically hold their own payer contract.

What makes their time billable is a chain: current RBT certification, an active treatment plan written by a credentialed BCBA, documented supervision, and correct claim submission under that payer's rules.

Break any link and the hours are not billable — even though the therapy was delivered competently.

## What RBTs actually need

**Current RBT certification.** Issued by the BACB following required training, a competency assessment, and an exam. Certification requires annual renewal and a competency re-assessment. Verify status at hire and monitor it. An expired RBT is an un-billable RBT.

**Documented ongoing supervision.** BACB standards require regular supervision by a qualified supervisor, including direct observation. Payers frequently impose their own supervision requirements on top, and those can be stricter than the certification standard.

**State requirements where they exist.** A growing number of states regulate behavior technicians or require registration. Check your state.

**Background screening.** Standard for anyone working with children, and often required by Medicaid and MCO contracts.

**Payer roster listing…sometimes.** Some payers do require technicians to be listed or registered with the plan, even without full credentialing. Ask each payer specifically rather than assuming.

## How technician time gets billed

The technician codes are **97153** (individual direct treatment by protocol), **97154** (group), and **97152** (supporting assessment). 97153 is the volume driver in most practices.

Submission mechanics vary by payer, and there is no national standard:

**Most payers** want technician services billed under the practice and the supervising BCBA, on the same claim, with a modifier identifying that a technician rendered the service.

**Some payers** (TRICARE is the frequently cited example) require technician services on a separate claim with a different modifier.

Getting this wrong produces denials that look like coding errors but are actually structural. Confirm each payer's ABA billing guidelines before your first submission, and re-confirm when guidelines update.

## The rule that creates real exposure

The compliance risk in ABA is not usually the RBT. It is the **BCBA whose NPI appears on the claim.**

That BCBA must be credentialed with the payer. A BCBA who is not yet credentialed, providing supervision or protocol modification that gets billed under a credentialed colleague's NPI, is a recognized audit red flag.

When an audit finds services rendered by non-credentialed providers, the payer can demand repayment of all associated paid claims, potentially going back years. Optum has explicitly stated that its supervisory billing allowances do not apply to ABA, closing a workaround some practices assumed was available.

So the practical control is upstream of the technician: **no BCBA renders billable services, including supervision billed as 97155, until their credentialing with that payer is complete.**

## Supervision documentation

This is where clean clinical records still fail the billing test.

Document, per session:

- **Who rendered the service. N**ame and credential.
- **Start and stop times.** Missing timestamps are among the most common audit failures.
- **The treatment plan and specific targets** the session worked from.
- **Data collected** and the client's response.
- **The supervision relationship** between technician and directing BCBA, where the payer requires it.

And for any concurrent 97153 and 97155 billing: both providers' distinct activities, each with their own timestamps, plus the payer-required modifier.

A note that reads well clinically but omits start and stop times will not survive an audit.

## What RBTs cannot do

- **Bill independently.** No own payer contract in the typical model.
- **Deliver services without an active treatment plan** written by a qualified BCBA.
- **Modify the protocol.** Protocol modification is 97155, and it is BCBA work. An RBT adjusting the plan independently is both a clinical and a billing problem.
- **Work without supervision** at the required frequency and structure.
- **Bill beyond authorized units.** Authorization caps apply regardless of who rendered the service.

## What practices should operationalize

**Track RBT certification like a credential.** Expiration dates, renewal, competency re-assessment in the same system as licenses and DEA registrations.

**Track supervision as a compliance requirement**, not just a clinical practice. Log it contemporaneously.

**Confirm each payer's technician billing rule** and document it where billing staff will see it.

**Gate BCBA scheduling on credentialing status.** This single control prevents the most expensive failure mode in ABA.

**Track authorized units per child** continuously against units delivered.

## FAQ

**Do RBTs get their own payer contracts?**

Usually no. Their services are billed under the practice and supervising BCBA.

**Do any payers require RBT enrollment?**

Some require technicians to be listed or registered. Ask each payer.

**Can RBT time be billed without supervision?**

No. Supervision at the required frequency is a condition of billing.

**Which code covers RBT direct therapy?**

97153 for individual, 97154 for group, 97152 for supporting assessment.

**Can an RBT modify the treatment plan?**

No. Protocol modification is BCBA work billed as 97155.

**What happens if an RBT's certification lapses?**

Their time is not billable. Track certification like any other credential.

**What's the biggest audit risk in ABA?**

Services billed under a BCBA who was not credentialed with that payer.

**Do we need timestamps on every note?**

Yes. Missing start and stop times is among the most common reasons clean clinical notes fail a billing audit.

The takeaway: RBTs are certified and supervised rather than independently credentialed; but the supervision, documentation, and BCBA credentialing rules behind their hours are strict, and that is where the money is won or lost.
