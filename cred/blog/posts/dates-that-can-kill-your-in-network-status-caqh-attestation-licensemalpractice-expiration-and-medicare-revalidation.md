Quick answer: Three recurring deadlines can end your in-network status without a phone call: (1) CAQH re-attestation, required roughly every 120 days; (2) license and malpractice expiration dates, which invalidate everything downstream; and (3) Medicare revalidation, due every 3–5 years with no extensions. Missing any of them can mean retroactive denied claims.

You did the hard part. You survived the 90-day credentialing gauntlet, signed the contracts, and started billing. But credentialing isn't a one-time event. It's a subscription you pay in deadlines, and the payers are not going to remind you.

## #1: CAQH re-attestation (~every 120 days)

CAQH ([https://proview.caqh.org](https://proview.caqh.org)) requires you to log in and re-attest that your profile data is current roughly every 120 days. It sounds trivial. It is not.

- When your attestation lapses, payers that pull from CAQH (which is most of them) treat your data as stale.
- Some pause re-credentialing, some suspend directory listings, and some quietly flag you for network termination.
- The email reminders go to whatever address you used when you created the profile, possibly years ago.

Set recurring calendar events and attest early. Update the profile email to one you actually check. Working with a managed service like Gusty Group can make sure no provider attestation date gets missed.

## #2: License and malpractice expirations

Your state license and malpractice policy are the foundation every payer contract sits on. When either expires even briefly or because a renewal payment processed late:

- Every payer relationship downstream is technically invalidated.
- Claims for dates of service in the gap can be denied and, worse, clawed back later during audits.
- Reinstating with a payer after a lapse can mean full re-credentialing, not a quick fix.

The fix: calendar alerts at 90, 60, and 30 days before every license and policy expiration. Upload renewals to CAQH the day you receive them. Payers verify against CAQH, not against your framed certificate. Gusty Group has the automations and hands on follow ups with your team to manage the full process.

## Date #3: Medicare revalidation (every 3–5 years, zero forgiveness)

Medicare ([https://www.cms.gov/medicare/enrollment-renewal/providers-suppliers/revalidation](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/enrollment-renewal/providers-suppliers/revalidation)) requires enrolled providers to revalidate every 5 years (3 for DME suppliers). This is the deadline with the sharpest teeth:

- Due dates are posted on CMS's revalidation lookup tool roughly 7 months in advance; but only if you check.
- There are no extensions. Miss it and your enrollment is deactivated.
- Deactivation means $0 reimbursement for the entire gap. Not delayed payment, no payment. Reactivation does not always restore retroactive billing rights.

The fix: check the CMS revalidation list twice a year for every NPI in your practice, and treat the posted due date as 60 days earlier than it says. Or work with a managed provider like Gusty Group who’s sole job is to make sure these dates don’t get missed.

## The maintenance calendar to set up today (15 minutes)

- CAQH re-attestation - recurring every 100 days
- State license renewal - alerts at 90/60/30 days out
- Malpractice policy renewal - alerts at 90/60/30 days out
- Medicare revalidation check - recurring every 6 months
- DEA registration renewal (if applicable) - alert at 60 days out
- Payer re-credentialing cycles (typically every 3 years) - log each payer's cycle when you're first approved

## FAQ

**Will insurance companies notify me before terminating my network status?**

Sometimes by mail or fax to the address from your original application. In practice, most providers discover the problem when claims start denying.

**What happens if my CAQH attestation lapses for a week?**

Usually recoverable: log in, update, re-attest. But if a payer pulled your file during the lapse, you may already be flagged, and you won't know until re-credentialing.

**Can I get retroactive payment after Medicare deactivation?**

Generally no. The deactivation gap is not reimbursable, which is why this is the most financially dangerous deadline on this list.
